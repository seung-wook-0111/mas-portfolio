  • Using an effective qualitative research methodology that combines think-aloud subway navigation tasks (with and without smartphone assistance) and semi-structured interviews paired with comparative ranking of existing information designs, to uncover users’ navigation strategies, ways of interpreting information, and user preferences
  • Deriving a reusable design framework that reflects diverse user perspectives through qualitative coding
  • Reframing subway navigation from station-name listing to directional understanding based on how users actually make decisions
  • Translating qualitative user research from diverse user groups into intuitive, logical, and more universal direction sign elements (icons, layout, and visual distinguishers)
  • Observing that direction signs are often placed at tactile paving intersections and proposing them as navigational landmarks for visually impaired users

Background

The Seoul Metropolitan Subway is one of the world’s largest and most complex metro systems, with over 300 stations and an annual ridership exceeding 2 billion, thanks to its affordability and convenience.

However, despite its vital role as an accessible public transportation system, the Seoul Metropolitan Subway has faced persistent criticism for its information design. The lack of a unified design system and the top-down nature of the bureaucratic design process have left many users—especially newcomers to the giant metropolis, travelers, foreign residents, and individuals with disabilities—struggling to navigate the complex network, despite multiple efforts to improve the design system.

User Study Problem

With these challenges in mind, we launched the Seoul Subway Information System Redesign project. To identify users’ needs in detail, we formulated the following research question:

“What specific information and design elements do users need to successfully navigate the subway system?”

Qualitative User Study

We recruited 11 participants from various age groups, including four foreigners and three visually impaired individuals, and conducted qualitative user research.

First, we conducted a Think-Aloud + Observation session. Participants were asked to complete two subway journeys: one without using a smartphone—simulating scenarios where network access or battery is unavailable—and another using smartphone apps as they normally would. We accompanied participants throughout their journey, from locating the entrance and using apps and signage to plan their route, to navigating inside the station, boarding the subway, and reaching their destination. During the process, participants were instructed to verbalize which information design elements they referenced and how they made their navigation decisions.

Following the first session, we conducted semi-structured interviews. Participants were presented with various styles of information design available in Seoul Subway, such as signs and displays, and asked to rate them. The goal was to encourage participants to articulate their thoughts, discuss the strengths and weaknesses of the design to identify the elements most important to their subway navigation and riding experience.

Analysis & Design Framework

Afterward, four team members conducted a qualitative analysis of all the collected data, which included open, axial, and selective coding.

As a result, we developed a design framework that reflects users’ experiences and opinions, highlighting key elements that remain prominent across different information design mediums and demographic groups.

Design Problem

“How can we apply the design framework to improve the subway users’ experience?”

Target Issue

UMDS addresses key shortcomings in current metro signage, including the failure to display core information, poor readability, and an illogical design that often leads to user confusion.

  • Lack of Core Information   |   Users primarily need to know where the train is headed and where they should go. However, current signs list numerous station names, which can be overwhelming, especially for those unfamiliar with them.
  • Poor Readability   |   Inconsistent or incorrect separators create uncertainty about directions, and small text sizes make the signs difficult to read.
  • Illogical Design   |   The inconsistency in signage design further confuses users. Instead of emphasizing train direction and navigation, which is all the users need, many signs present excessive station names, reducing their efficiency.

Designing the Solution

UMDS was designed by directly applying the design framework.

Visualizing Train Directions   |   Instead of listing multiple station names, UMDS visually represents the train’s direction (upbound, downbound, clockwise, counterclockwise, etc). It also maintains a consistent layout by displaying a major destination along with the train’s next stop

Enhanced Readability   |   The support rod doubles as a visual separator to distinguish different directions, reducing confusion. Text and arrows are enlarged for better readability.

Logical Design Rules   |   UMDS follows specific design rules to enhance clarity. It prioritizes symbols over text, using icons such as an airplane for the Airport Railroad whenever possible. For exit numbers, if six or fewer exits are listed, all numbers are shown, whereas if there are more than six, they are displayed in a condensed “1 ᐧᐧᐧ 9” format for readability, reflecting user research results and Miller's Law.

Accessibility Features   |   Recognizing that signage is often placed at intersections where tactile paving cannot aid making directional decisions, UMDS proposes guide audio features at signs, serving as audible landmarks for visually impaired users.
